# LinkedIn posts — Recruiter Search Rank launch sequence

A single repeatable format, plus 5 first-week drafts (one per role
family). Every post points to a `/rank/<role>` SEO page on
hiring.productions — so the post does two jobs at once: builds Stephanie's
audience AND builds backlinks to the pages we want Google to index.

**Cadence**: 1 per week, Monday morning. Cycle through the 5 roles, then
repeat with a new angle.

**Why this format works**: it gives the audience a recruiter-side insight
they couldn't get anywhere else (real boolean search behavior), names the
fix specifically, and the link is to a *role-specific* page — not the
homepage. Specificity earns clicks. Specificity also tells Google these
pages are worth indexing.

**Voice rules** (from BRAND.md):
- No emojis
- No fabricated specifics — every number/quote has to come from a real run
- No hedging ("might help" → "fixes")
- "I" not "we" — it's Stephanie's voice
- Dry-funny okay. Performing expertise — not okay.

---

## The repeatable format

```
Hook line — something specific most recruiters won't say.
(One sentence. Sharp.)

The pattern in 2-3 sentences. Quote a real thing recruiters do — the
boolean string format, the way they scan headlines first, the way a
"Founder" current title disqualifies someone from every senior IC search.

The fix. One specific change. Not "optimize your profile" — actual words
to change.

One short bridge line.

If you want to see exactly where you rank when a recruiter searches
LinkedIn for [role]: hiring.productions/rank/[slug]

It's free to run. Built it because nobody outside recruiting can see this.

#hiring #careers #linkedin
```

Three rules for filling it in:

1. **The hook is half the post.** If the first line doesn't make a
   recruiter who already knows this stuff lean in, kill it and try again.
2. **Quote real syntax.** `"Senior Product Manager" AND SaaS` reads more
   credibly than "recruiters search exact titles." Show the string.
3. **The fix has to be specific.** "Move 'Senior PM' to your headline"
   beats "rewrite your headline to be more keyword-rich."

## Week 1 — Product Manager

> The boolean string a Series-B recruiter actually types when sourcing PMs is shorter than you think.
>
> It looks like this: `"Senior Product Manager" AND SaaS AND (analytics OR experimentation)`
>
> Nothing fancy. No "growth mindset" filter. No personality keywords. Just exact title, sector, and the two skills they need on day one.
>
> Which means: if your headline says "Senior PM driving 0-to-1 growth," that recruiter never sees you. Their query is matching the literal phrase "Senior Product Manager." You missed by one word.
>
> The fix is one line. Put "Senior Product Manager" — exactly — in your headline. Keep the rest of your story. Just lead with the phrase the search is matching on.
>
> If you want to see exactly where you rank in the 5 searches a recruiter for your PM role would actually run: hiring.productions/rank/product-manager
>
> Free to run. Built it because nobody outside recruiting can see this.

## Week 2 — Software Engineer

> When recruiters source senior engineers, the Skills section is the filter, not the bullets.
>
> A typical sourcing query looks like this:
> `(TypeScript OR Go) AND Kubernetes AND "distributed systems"`
>
> LinkedIn doesn't scan your job description bullets for those. It scans your Skills section — as exact strings. "Used Go in production for 3 years" in a bullet matches nothing. "Go" as a discrete Skill matches every query that filters on it.
>
> The fix: open your Skills section, add the literal stack you actually work in — language by language, framework by framework, infra term by infra term. Don't curate it. Don't be modest.
>
> If you want to see exactly where you rank in the 5 searches a recruiter sourcing engineers would actually run: hiring.productions/rank/software-engineer
>
> Free to run. Built by a senior TA director who hired engineers for a decade.
>
> #hiring #careers #linkedin

## Week 3 — Designer

> Recruiters who source for product design roles type that exact phrase. A profile that reads "Designer crafting human experiences" or "Creative Lead" never enters the result set — even when the actual work is identical.
>

## Week 4 — Marketer

> "Marketing Manager" is the worst headline in marketing.
>
> Not because it's wrong — but because it's so common that every senior marketer competing for the same role is also titled "Marketing Manager." You disappear into a pool of 2,000.

## Week 5 — Data Analyst

> The Skills section is doing more work for data candidates than the resume is.
>
> Sourcing queries for data roles in 2026 look like this:
> `"Data Analyst" AND SQL AND (Tableau OR Looker) AND dbt`
>
> Every term in that string is a Skills-section match, not an experience-bullet match. If "dbt" is buried inside a 2024 role description but not added to Skills, your profile fails the filter — even if you write dbt every day.
>
> Two-minute fix: open your Skills, add SQL, Python, dbt, Snowflake, Tableau or Looker, and the cloud warehouse you actually use. Don't list the ones you've heard of — list the ones you've shipped on.

## After week 5 — what to write next

Three repeatable angles that all route to the same `/rank/<role>` pages:

1. **The "Founder" trap** — a post about how having "Founder" or
   "Consultant" as your current title disqualifies you from every
   IC-role boolean search. Real, common, and the fix is to add an
   employee title alongside.

2. **The geography hard filter** — most candidates don't know that
   LinkedIn location is a hard filter, not a ranking signal. Wrong geo
   = excluded entirely. Tell the story of finding strong candidates who
   were invisible because they hadn't updated their location.

3. **The Open-to-Work paradox** — the green frame helps in active-
   candidate search and quietly hurts in passive-candidate search at
   some firms. Counter-intuitive, recruiter-specific, and the kind of
   thing only a real TA director would say out loud.

Each of those, paired with the `/rank/<role>` link, gives Stephanie
roughly 15 high-leverage posts before she has to find a new angle.

## What NOT to post

- "Excited to announce…" anything. Hiring.productions earns its audience
  with insight, not announcements.
- Listicles ("10 LinkedIn tips"). Generic listicles match a thousand
  career coaches. The voice should sound like Stephanie reading a
  profile out loud.
- Anything implying the simulator gives an exact rank. It's an
  *estimate* and the result page is clear about that. The posts should
  match.
- Fabricated client stories. Anonymized real examples only. If a story
  has to be invented to make the post work, the post isn't ready.
